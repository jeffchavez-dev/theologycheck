One of the most misunderstood doctrines in contemporary theology is divine simplicity — the teaching that God is not composed of parts, that His essence and existence are identical, and that His attributes are not really distinct from one another or from His essence. This is the historic [classical theism](https://theologycheckblog.wordpress.com/2025/10/08/what-is-classical-theism-2/).

## What Is Divine Simplicity?

The doctrine of divine simplicity (DDS) holds that God is not a composite being. He does not have parts — whether physical, metaphysical, or logical. His goodness is not something added to His essence; rather, God *is* His goodness. His wisdom, power, and love are not distinct properties that God happens to possess — they are all identical with His one, simple, undivided essence.

As Thomas Aquinas writes in the *Summa Theologiae*:

> "God is simple, because He is not composed of matter and form, nor does He have accidents, nor is He composed of essence and existence."

## Is This a Greek Imposition?

A common objection — especially in evangelical and post-conservative circles — is that divine simplicity is an illegitimate import from Platonic or Aristotelian philosophy that distorts the biblical portrait of God.

This objection, however, fails on historical grounds. The doctrine is affirmed not only by Aquinas but by the entire patristic tradition — Athanasius, Augustine, John of Damascus — and is explicitly confessed in the Reformed confessions.

The *Westminster Confession of Faith* (2.1) describes God as "most holy, most free, most absolute, working all things according to the counsel of His own immutable and most holy will… without body, parts, or passions."

The *London Baptist Confession of 1689* (2.1) echoes this precisely.

## Why It Matters

Divine simplicity is not an abstract philosophical curiosity. It is the doctrine that protects the Creator-creature distinction. If God were composed of parts — if His love were one thing and His justice another — then He would be dependent on something outside Himself for His composition. He would not be the ultimate, self-sufficient, self-existent Being.

The God of Scripture is *aseity* itself — existence from Himself, dependent on nothing. And this is precisely what divine simplicity protects.

For the Reformed Baptist, the recovery of classical theism is not a departure from confessional theology — it is a return to it.
