## Preface

This book seeks to develop and present the covenant theology of the Bible through a thorough study of the Scriptures.

---

## Chapter 1: Biblical Theology and Systematic Theology in Covenant Theology

**Introduction** — Covenant theology is not an easy subject to study. *(Romans 11:33, 36; Proverbs 24:27)*

**1. Scope and Simplicity** — The first challenge to a proper balance between biblical theology and systematic theology in covenant theology is that of scope and simplicity.

**2. Creation, Covenant, and Consequences** — The second challenge is the fundamental distinction between creation and covenant. *(Luke 17:10; Acts 17:24–25; Job 35:5–8; Job 41:11; Romans 2:14–15; Hebrews 9:10; 1 Corinthians 7:19; Philippians 4:8–9; Romans 4:11)*

**3. The Law and the Gospel** — Covenant theology must pay attention to biblical theology's progress and specifics, and then build a system that reflects this. *(Romans 11:6; Ephesians 2:8; Galatians 3:2; Genesis 3:15)*

**4. History and Mystery** — Closely connected to the consideration of the law and the gospel as doctrinal opposites and successive time periods is the acknowledgement of the relation between history and mystery. *(Ephesians 3:4; Colossians 4:3; Colossians 1:25–27; Ephesians 3:4–12; Romans 16:25–26; Ephesians 1:9–10; Acts 1:6; Luke 24:25–27; Acts 28:23)*

**Conclusion** — These four methodological considerations are necessary foundations for the relationship of biblical theology and systematic theology in covenant theology. *(Romans 11:33–36)*

---

## Chapter 2: Typology

**Introduction** — Typology is one of the most important pieces of biblical interpretation in general, as well as the mystery of Christ and covenant theology in particular.

**1. What is Typology?** — Though typology often appears in theological literature, there are distinct differences in the way that authors use typology, whether today, or in the past.

**2. The Relation between a Type and its Antitype** — These principles are helpful in developing our understanding of a typological hermeneutic, but they are incomplete.

- **a. Types reveal something greater, and other, than themselves.** A cursory definition of typology as correspondence and escalation may give the impression that typology is simply a shift from one end of a scale to another. *(John 6:32–33; 1 Corinthians 10:4; Colossians 2:17; Hebrews 8:5; 9:23–24; 10:1)*

- **b. Types function on two levels.** Typology functions on two levels. Types may be temporary and preparatory for antitypes, but it is necessary to appreciate their own meaning and purpose in their own context.

- **c. Types terminate in their antitypes.** If types are not their antitypes, it naturally follows that when the antitype arrives, the type is discarded.

- **d. Types are positive and negative.** Types reveal antitypes that are greater and other than themselves. *(Jeremiah 31:31–32; Hebrews 10:1)*

**3. The Application of Typological Principles** — Applying the principles of typology is worked out in the rest of this book.

**Conclusion** — Small differences in the application of typology often lead to greater distances than one might suspect.

## Chapter 3: Covenant and Kingdom

**Introduction** — Establishing fundamental definitions of important terms and concepts is a necessary part of developing a methodological toolkit.

- **a. The definition of a covenant** — A covenant (*berith* in Hebrew, *diathēkē* in Greek) is a guaranteed commitment.

- **b. Covenant sanctions** — Covenant sanctions are threats that enforce and ensure the fulfillment of the covenantal commitments. *(Jeremiah 34:18–20; Psalm 50:5; 1 Samuel 20:12–16; Genesis 15:9–10; Hebrews 9:16–17)*

- **c. The matter and form of a covenant** — Covenants are built out of commitments and oaths that protect those commitments. *(Romans 11:6; Romans 4:4–5; Galatians 3:18; Genesis 17:14; Exodus 24:7–8)*

- **d. Federal headship** — An essential feature of federal theology is that of federal headship. *(Hebrews 7:9–10; Matthew 3:9)*

**2. What is a Covenant's Function?** — In this section we will tie together various strands of thought in a simple way. *(Psalm 24:1–2; Psalm 33:13–15; Daniel 4:34–35; Ephesians 1:9–10)*

**Conclusion** — In summary, a covenant is a divinely sanctioned commitment defining the relationship between God and another party.

---

## Chapter 4: The Covenant of Works

**Introduction** — The biblical doctrine of the Covenant of Works depends on the distinction between that which was natural to Adam at creation, and that which was granted beyond nature.

**1. Man's Created Condition** — Creation, distinct from the Creator, is good. God declared it to be good seven times in Genesis 1, summing this up in a declaration of exceeding goodness in Genesis 1:31.

**2. Man's Covenantal Condition** — A covenant is a commitment with divine sanctions. God placed Adam under commitments beyond his created obligations and added sanctions to guarantee Adam's participation.

- **a. God placed Adam in the garden of Eden** — Eden was not simply another part of the world; it was God's dwelling place. *(Genesis 2:8–9, 15; Hebrews 12:22; Exodus 25:31–39)*

- **b. God appointed Adam federal head over his natural offspring** — Adam was a federal head by God's appointment. *(Romans 5:19; 1 Corinthians 15:22; Genesis 1:28)*

- **c. God obligated Adam to a law of obedience** — By nature, Adam owed absolute obedience to God. Beyond the law of nature written on Adam's heart, God gave Adam a positive law of obedience. *(Genesis 2:15–17; Numbers 3:5–10)*

- **d. God promised eternal life to Adam** — The most prominent feature of the garden was its two trees, the tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. *(Revelation 2:7; Genesis 3:22–24; Romans 3:23; Hebrews 2:10)*

- **e. God threatened Adam with sanctions** — Sanctions formalize covenantal commitments. God clearly threatened Adam with death if he disobeyed. *(Genesis 2:17)*

- **f. God made a Covenant of Works with Adam** — When God's dealings with Adam beyond that which was natural to him are considered, it is clear that God made a covenant of works with Adam.

- **g. God tested Adam's obedience** — The Covenant of Works was not an unbounded endless demand for obedience in the garden. *(Romans 3:23; Romans 5:17–18)*

**3. Man's Cursed Condition** — God told Adam that on the day he ate of the fruit of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, he would surely die.

- **a. Death** — As a federal head, Adam was a representative of his natural offspring in the Covenant of Works. *(Romans 5:12; 1 Corinthians 15:21–22; Ephesians 2:1–3; 4:18; 1 Corinthians 2:14; Matthew 7:17–18; Romans 5:6; Titus 3:3; Genesis 3:22)*

- **b. The abrogation of the covenant of works** — Adam's breach of the covenant activated its curses, expelled him from the temple of God's presence, and condemned him to death.

- **c. A merciful curse** — God did not exact the full rigor of the curse upon Adam and Eve immediately upon their disobedience. *(Genesis 3:15)*

**4. The Protological Nature of the Kingdom** — The Covenant of Works is protological. Protology is the establishment of foundational symbols, concepts, and constructs that are repeated and reused by God in His Word.

**Conclusion** — In the Covenant of Works God delegated dominion over Eden to Adam through covenant, making him a king of that realm.

## Chapter 5: The Noahic Covenant

**Introduction** — The Covenant of Works has all creation in view. So does the Noahic Covenant.

**1. A New Creation and Commission** — Moses composed this account of Noah with language that treats it as a new creation account. *(Genesis 8:21; 9:1–2)*

**2. A Judicial Retribution** — God establishes and governs His kingdoms through covenant. *(Genesis 9:5–7)*

**3. A Promised Preservation** — Covenants are often reducible to "I will" and "you will" statements. *(Genesis 9:8–17; 8:22)*

**4. A Common Cursed Kingdom of Common Grace** — Putting all the pieces together yields the conclusion that the kingdom of creation is a common cursed kingdom of common grace.

**Conclusion** — Kingdom and covenant give us a clear and concrete way not just of describing the world in convenient terms, but of defining the world biblically.

---

## Chapter 6: The Abrahamic Covenant

**Introduction** — Up to this point in our study the nation of Israel did not exist.

**1. The Initiation of the Covenant** — The beginnings of the covenant God made with Abraham are found in Genesis 12:1–7. *(Genesis 12:1–7)*

- **a. The federal head** — God always transacts His covenants through federal heads. *(Genesis 12:2)*
- **b. The inheritance of Canaan** — God promised to Abraham that he and his descendants would inherit the land of Canaan.
- **c. The blessing for the nations** — The Israelite kingdom and its covenants are preparatory and subservient to God's final purposes. *(Ephesians 1:10; 3:8–12)*

**2. The Confirmation of the Covenant** — The covenant initiated in Genesis 12 through promises is confirmed in Genesis 15. *(Genesis 15:18)*

**3. The Expansion of the Covenant** — It is important to note the continuity of promises between Genesis 12, 15, and 17. *(Genesis 17:4–8; 17:6; 35:11; 49:8–10; 17:14)*

**4. The Realization of the Covenant** — The Scriptures carefully record God's fulfillment of His promises. *(Joshua 21:43–45; Nehemiah 9:7–8; 1 Kings 4:20; Hebrews 11:12; Luke 1:54–55, 68)*

**5. The Foundation of the Old Covenant** — Genesis 12–17 establishes a foundation for redemptive history until the advent of Christ. *(Genesis 28:4; 35:12; Exodus 6:2–4; Deuteronomy 1:8; Joshua 1:6; Psalm 105:6–11; Romans 11:1; Acts 3:25–26)*

- **a.** The Abrahamic Covenant anticipates the Mosaic Covenant — in that it tells the Israelites that they will be disinherited if they don't keep the covenant. *(Genesis 15:13)*
- **b.** The Abrahamic Covenant anticipates the Davidic Covenant — including a promise of royalty to both Abraham and Sarah.
- **c.** The Abrahamic Covenant anticipates the New Covenant — in two ways. *(Romans 4:10; Hebrews 11:9–10)*
- **d.** The Abrahamic Covenant echoes Eden — typology develops progressively; what was established protologically in Eden with Adam is picked up and expanded here.

**Conclusion** — In successive stages, God made a covenant with Abraham, promising him that his multiplied offspring would inherit Canaan and live there under their own rulers after a period of several hundred years of sojourning and slavery.

---

## Chapter 7: The Mosaic Covenant

**Introduction** — The source of God's first dealings with Abraham was the infinite goodness and kindness of God.

- **a.** The promise of oppression and affliction *(Genesis 15:13–16; Exodus 2:23–25; 12:40–41)*
- **b.** The promise of liberation and fulfilment *(Exodus 3:13–17; 6:1–5; Psalm 77:20)*

**2. The Kind of Covenant** — The Mosaic Covenant is a development of the Abrahamic Covenant in which God made promises He would fulfill nationally to Abraham's descendants, while they must keep the covenant if they want to enjoy its benefits and blessings.

- **a. Its laws** *(Exodus 19:3–8; 24:3–8; Hebrews 9:18–23; Exodus 34:27–28)*
- **b. Its promises** *(Jeremiah 11:2–5; 7:3–7; Exodus 32:13)*
- **c. Its threats** *(Deuteronomy 6:20; 11:26–28; 27:26; 29:22–28; 30:15–20)*

**3. The Kindness of the Covenant** — The Mosaic Covenant is a covenant of obedience for life in the land of Canaan.

- **a. The absolute dominion of God** — God's kindness in the Mosaic Covenant is visible in comparison with His absolute dominion.
- **b. The promises to Abraham** *(Exodus 32:13–14)*
- **c. The sacrificial system** *(Leviticus 5:14–19; 16:21–22)*
- **d. The history of Israel** *(Deuteronomy 4:25–31; Nehemiah 9:7–8)*

**4. The Function of the Covenant** — A kingdom is a sphere of sovereignty, granted by God.

- **a.** The covenant governs the people *(Deuteronomy 4:1–2)*
- **b.** The covenant governs the priesthood *(Exodus 40:12–16; Leviticus 10:1–2)*
- **c.** The covenant governs the kingship *(Deuteronomy 17:14–20)*
- **d.** The covenant governs the prophets *(Numbers 12:6; Deuteronomy 13:1–5)*
- **e.** The covenant governs blessing and cursing *(Deuteronomy 27:9–10; 28:1–2; 31:9–13; 32:45–47)*

**Conclusion** — The Mosaic Covenant controls everything. It governs the people in its various kinds of laws; it governs the priests, the kings, and the prophets. *(Hebrews 10:1)*

---

## Chapter 8: The Davidic Covenant

**Introduction** — The Kingdom of Israel was established and ruled by covenants.

- **a.** The promises of the Abrahamic Covenant *(Genesis 17:6)*
- **b.** The problems of the Mosaic Covenant *(Judges 2:10–11; 21:25)*
- **c.** The preference of the people *(1 Samuel 8:7–9)*
- **d.** The prospect of consummation *(Deuteronomy 12:8–12)*

**2. The Blessings of the Covenant** — The Bible records the covenant God made with David in 2 Samuel 7:8–16.

- **a.** An established throne — David's house, kingdom, and throne will be established and made sure.
- **b.** Rest and prosperity in Canaan *(1 Kings 8:56; Psalm 144:1–2)*
- **c.** The presence and protection of God *(1 Kings 6:12–13)*

**3. The Conditions of the Covenant** — The Davidic Covenant was not comprised simply of God's promises to David, but also of God's commands to David and his descendants.

- **a.** Guard God's sanctuary *(2 Samuel 7:13)*
- **b.** Keep God's law
- **c.** Represent God's people

**4. The Sanctions of the Covenant** — The Davidic king was a federal head or representative for the nation, whose obedience or disobedience determined the blessing or cursing of the people.

- **a.** The judgment of God himself *(2 Samuel 7:14; Psalm 132:11–12; 1 Kings 8:25–26)*
- **b.** Expulsion from the land of Canaan *(1 Kings 9:4–9; 11:11–13; 2 Kings 23:26–27)*

**Conclusion** — With the Abrahamic, Mosaic, and Davidic Covenants put together we can clearly see the kingdom of Israel in full.

---

## Chapter 9: The Messiah of the Old Covenant

**Introduction** — The primary concern of this chapter is the development of the central concept of the Messiah to the kingdom of Israel and its covenants.

**1. The Messianic Purpose of the Old Covenant** — From its inception in Genesis 12, the Israelite kingdom and its covenants contained the promise of a blessing-mediating descendant. *(Romans 9:4; Ephesians 2:12; Hebrews 10:1)*

**2. The Messianic Hope of the Old Covenant** — The messianic hope of the Old Covenant derives directly from the Davidic Covenant.

- **a. The present Messiah** *(Psalm 18:50; 20:6–9; 132:10–18)*
- **b. The future Messiah** — The higher you climb, the farther you fall. Israel's hopes climbed high, due to God's promises. *(Hosea 6:7; Psalm 89:28–49; Jeremiah 33:14–18; Isaiah 9:1–7)*

**3. The Messianic Promise of a New Covenant** — The imperfections of the Israelite kingdom and its covenants, combined with the promises of God, produced a longing for something other and greater than what Israel experienced. *(Ezekiel 37:24–28; Jeremiah 31:31–34)*

**4. The Messianic Inclusion of the Nations** — In Genesis 12, God had promised that Abraham's line would produce a descendant through whom a blessing for the world would come. *(Micah 4:1–4; Isaiah 2:2–4; Haggai 2:6–9; Isaiah 11:1–10)*

**5. The Typological Nature of the Kingdom** — The kingdom of Israel and its covenants were typical shadows of Christ, the substance. *(Luke 1:26–33; Mark 1:14–15; John 1:41)*

**Conclusion** — In a world cursed through Adam and stabilized through Noah, a special kingdom was formed through covenants made with Abraham and his descendants in Canaan.

---

## Chapter 10: The Ministry of the Christ

**Introduction** — Israel was longing and looking for a Davidic descendant whose throne would be established by God Himself in such a way that they would be freed from foreign powers and restored to the former glory of the kingdom.

**1. The Gospel of the Kingdom** — From the beginning of the Christ's ministry, He declared good news (gospel). *(Mark 1:14–15; Luke 4:18; Matthew 11:28–30; Matthew 9:11–12; Mark 1:34; John 10:14–18; Ezekiel 34:22–25; John 11:25–27)*

**2. The Kingdom of Heaven** — The kingdom proclaimed by the Christ is a kingdom not of this world. *(John 18:36; 3:3–6; Matthew 8:11–12; John 8:39; Matthew 16:16–19; 18:1–4)*

**3. The King of the Kingdom** — The Christ, Jesus, claimed absolute authority in the kingdom of heaven. *(Matthew 5:17–20; 21:9)*

**Conclusion** — The ministry of Jesus Christ initiated the unveiling of the full and final plan of God.

---

## Chapter 11: The Covenant of Redemption

**Introduction** — The New Covenant announced by the Messiah was established on a preceding covenantal foundation. *(2 Timothy 1:9; Titus 1:2)*

**1. The Parties of the Covenant** — The parties of the covenant of redemption are the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. *(Isaiah 42:1–7; 49:8–9; 50:4–9; Luke 4:17–21)*

- **a. The Son's commitments** — In the Servant Songs of Isaiah, God the Father gave a mission to His Servant. *(John 10:17–18; Psalm 2:6–9; 110:4; Hebrews 7:28; Isaiah 61:1–2; John 17:8)*

- **b. The Father's commitments** *(Isaiah 42:6; 49:8; 50:7; Luke 1:35; Matthew 12:27–28; Hebrews 9:14; Isaiah 53:10–12; Philippians 2:8–11; 1 John 3:8)*

**3. The Fulfillment of the Covenant** — God the Son willingly took up the mission given to Him. *(Philippians 2:5–8; Isaiah 50:5–7; 53:10–12; Acts 2:32–33; Hebrews 10:12–14; Revelation 5:12)*

**Conclusion** — The Covenant of Redemption is a wonderful doctrine.

---

## Chapter 12: The New Covenant of Grace

**Introduction** — Jesus Christ is the son of Abraham and David, the one through whom the nations of the world are blessed. *(Matthew 1:1; Luke 1:68–75; Galatians 4:4–5)*

**1. The Establishment of the New Covenant** — The writer to the Hebrews argues that the New Covenant is a better covenant than the Old because it was legally established on superior promises. *(Hebrews 8:6; 9:11–15; Ephesians 2:16; Colossians 1:20–22; Hebrews 9:24–26)*

**2. The Blessings of the New Covenant** — The New Covenant announced by Jesus Christ on the night of His betrayal is the New Covenant of Jeremiah 31. *(Jeremiah 31:31–34; Hebrews 8:8–13)*

- **a. Justification** — Through one act of disobedience, Adam brought condemnation on all his people. *(Romans 5:17–19; Hebrews 10:14; Psalm 103:12; Isaiah 53:5; Hebrews 9:9–10, 13–14; Isaiah 53:11; Romans 5:18–19; 10:9–13)*

- **b. Regeneration and Sanctification** — The New Covenant is better than the Old not only because it promises lasting, perfect, heavenly forgiveness, but also because it promises to perform an inward work of renewal in the people of God. *(Jeremiah 31:33–34; Ezekiel 36:26–27; John 3:3; Romans 6:17–18; Ephesians 2:1–10; 2 Corinthians 3:5–9; Romans 10:17; James 1:18; 1 Peter 1:23)*

- **c. Adoption and Preservation** — The people of the New Covenant are the children of God. *(John 1:12–13; Romans 8:15; 1 John 3:1–2; Hebrews 12:3–11; Jeremiah 32:40)*

- **d. Resurrection and Glorification** — The Servant of Jehovah was promised in Isaiah 53 that if He poured out His life for His people, His days would be prolonged, He would see His offspring, and He would be exalted. *(Psalm 16:10; Romans 4:25; John 6:39–40; Hebrews 2:10; Colossians 1:18; 1 Corinthians 15:45; Revelation 22:4; Psalm 73:23–28; 16:11)*

**3. The Foundation of the New Covenant** — The Covenant of Redemption is the foundation of the New Covenant. *(Hebrews 7:22; Ephesians 1:11; Hebrews 9:15; Romans 8:9; 1 John 5:11–13; Hebrews 6:13–20; Jeremiah 31:32; Ephesians 2:8–9)*

**4. The Kingdom of the New Covenant** — Kingdom and covenant always go hand in hand. *(Colossians 1:18; Luke 22:29)*

**5. The People of the New Covenant** — The membership of a given covenant is always determined by the federal head of that covenant. *(Romans 8:1, 9, 14, 17, 29–30, 31, 33–39; Colossians 1:12–14)*

**Conclusion** — The New Covenant of grace, established in the blood of Christ, founded in the Covenant of Redemption, and preached to the world in the gospel, is God's master plan.

## Chapter 13: The Mystery of Christ

**Introduction** — From "before" creation, salvation in Christ for all nations had been planned. *(2 Timothy 1:9; Titus 1:2; Romans 1:16; Acts 1:6)*

**1. The Unity of God's Plan** — God has only ever had one plan, and history played out according to God's design. *(Ephesians 1:9–10; Acts 2:38–39; Colossians 1:25–28; 2:4–23; Ephesians 3:1–6; 1 Peter 1:10–12)*

**2. Israel, Christ, and the Church** — The kingdom and covenant of Christ declared by Jesus Himself during His ministry were neither defined nor determined by Jewish ancestry and obedience to Jewish laws.

- **a. Israel** — Israel is the natural offspring of Abraham. *(Romans 11:1; Romans 4:1; 2 Corinthians 11:22; Romans 9:3–5; Ephesians 2:12; Romans 10:4; Romans 4:9–17; John 8:37, 39; Romans 9:8; Galatians 3:7, 16–17; 4:21–31)*

- **b. The Christ** — God's faithfulness to Israel is demonstrated above all in Jesus Christ. *(Matthew 1:1; Romans 1:16; 11:1–10, 23)*

- **c. The Church** — The apostles used Psalm 118:22–23 to describe the transition from Israel to the church, centered around Jesus Christ. *(Psalm 118:22–23; Ephesians 2:19–22; 1 Peter 2:4–10; John 1:12–13; Philippians 3:3; Colossians 1:18; Galatians 3:26–28; Romans 11:16–24; Galatians 6:16; 2 Corinthians 5:16–17; Hebrews 8:13)*

**3. The Continuity of the Law and the Gospel** — Salvation for the nations was promised to Abraham and his descendants. *(John 8:56; Romans 4:6–8; 1 Corinthians 10:1–4; Galatians 3:8; Hebrews 9:25–26; Psalm 32:1; Romans 4:7–8; Matthew 5:20–24; Romans 2:14–15; 13:8–10; 1 John 5:1–3; Jeremiah 31:33)*

**Conclusion** — Paul prayed for wisdom to explain the mystery of Christ, the connection of the people of God according to the flesh and the people of God according to the Spirit with the Christ at the center. *(Colossians 4:3–4; Romans 11:33–36)*

---

## Chapter 14: The Eschatological Nature of the Kingdom

**Introduction** — The kingdom that Adam should have built, but failed to build, and the kingdom that Israel was designed to prefigure and prepare — that kingdom was the Kingdom of Heaven, the Kingdom of God, the Kingdom of Christ.

**1. The Kingdom Inaugurated** — The church is the kingdom of Christ in the present age. *(Romans 10:14–15; 2 Corinthians 5:20; Revelation 2:5; Romans 8:9; John 3:3)*

**2. The Keys of the Kingdom** — Because the church is the future in the present, the final kingdom already inaugurated, the value and meaning of joining and leaving the visible kingdom take on equal, but opposite, values.

- **a. Joining the kingdom through the covenant** *(1 Peter 4:3; Romans 10:9; Hebrews 9:15)*

- **b. Guarding the kingdom through the covenant** *(1 John 1:10; Hebrews 12:14; John 3:18)*

- **c. Traitors of the kingdom** — How, then, are we to view apostates, those who fully and finally reject the kingdom? *(1 John 2:19; Acts 8:13; 2 Peter 2:1; Hebrews 6:4–6; Psalm 50:16)*

**3. The Sacraments of the Kingdom** — Christ has given His church visible tangible means of participating in His promises.

- **a. Baptism** — Baptism is a visible word, a representation, of new creation life through death. *(Romans 6:3–5; 1 Peter 3:21–22)*

- **b. The Lord's Supper** — The bread and wine of the Lord's Supper are visible words of Christ's New Covenant pledges to His people. *(Hebrews 10:23–25)*

**4. The Kingdom Consummated** — We proclaim the Lord's death until He comes. When Jesus Christ returns, He will bring all things to consummation. *(Acts 17:30–31; Psalm 2:12; 2 Peter 3:15; 2 Thessalonians 1:4–10; Hebrews 9:27–28; Matthew 25:31–46; Revelation 20:15; 1 Corinthians 15:24; Revelation 21:1–7; 21:23; 5:9–10; 22:4; 22:16–18)*

**Conclusion** — The kingdom of Christ is an inaugurated manifestation of the eschatological kingdom of God. *(Romans 9:5)*

---

## Conclusion

God covenanted a kingdom to Adam, and Adam set it on fire. But in Christ, the last Adam, the whole purpose of God is brought to its glorious end.

*(Romans 8:32; Hebrews 6:19; Romans 10:11–13; Ephesians 3:14–21)*
