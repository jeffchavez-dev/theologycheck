If God is impassible, how can he truly know his creatures? If God, before the incarnation, did not experience our experience on earth, how can he sympathize with his servants? Here is a quick post drawn from the latest Theology in Particular Podcast. I glean good quotes and notes from James Dolezal's discussion of Impassibility and the knowledge of God.[^1]

> "There are no states of being in God." —James Dolezal

> "Knowledge is in the knower according to the mode of the knower's being or nature... God knows divinely. God's knowledge is superior to our way of knowing. So while not having experiential knowledge, he has a knowledge of experiential knowledge that is actually deeper and more luminous and penetrating than even experiential knowledge itself." —James Dolezal

## How is it that the impassible God knows His creatures?

Simply because God is the cause of all things. His knowledge is the knowledge of the universal, complete, and perfectly sufficient cause of all things. To know things best is to know why. To paraphrase Aristotle, "a thing is known best when it is known in its cause."

God knows things in principle. He knows why. To know the why is deeper. He doesn't only know "that" the world but he knows "why" the world. In fact, He 'is' why the world. God knows the world best because he knows the world in its deepest causal principle which is himself. God's knowledge as the absolute, universal, and complete cause of all that comes to be and the immediate sustainer of all things, we can say that God knows the world best because he best knows the world why.

God's knowledge is not merely experiential or propositional. There is a knowledge that transcends both those ways of knowing, which is causal, the knowledge of the Artisan, the knowledge of the Maker of all. God knows all things best because he knows them in their deepest causal analysis. He knows Himself as their cause.

God knows our experience, though not experientially. God has a non-experiential knowledge of our experiences.

> God doesn't need to have an empathic experience of the creatures in order to come near and to care. God knows our knowledge even better than we know it. There is a knowledge that is even more intrinsic and penetrating than first-hand experiential knowledge, and it is the knowledge of the One who is creating and causing that knowledge... In Him, we live, and move, and have our being (Acts 17:28). —James Dolezal

Dolezal concludes this podcast by reflecting on the comforting truth that God knows us better than we know ourselves. When we are suffering, He knows our hearts. He knows the reason why we are suffering. God knows us even when no one seems to understand. That's why we can trust Him. Added to this, He has given us many precious promises and demonstrations of His love and care for us. While God *as God* is impassible, the incarnate Son, as man, is fully capable of sympathy and has directly experienced human weaknesses and suffering for our sake. Indeed, in Christ, the impassible suffered.[^2]

**Bonus:** At around the 20-minute mark, Dolezal provides a very good explanation of Acts 14:14-15. Go check it!

To the Triune God alone be the glory!
