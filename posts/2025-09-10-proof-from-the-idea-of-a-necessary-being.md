This proof is commonly called the *a priori* proof for God's existence, known as a deductive argument from divine existence, and the ontological proof in a strict sense, in its historic and classic form. From Greek philosophers (Aristotle and Plato), to the early church Fathers (Tertullian and Augustine) laying the foundation, to the Scholastics (Anselm and Aquinas) elaborating this proof, to Descartes giving prominence to the Anselmian form, we see the inseparability of *a priori* and *a posteriori* proofs contained in the ontological argument.

The argument for God's necessary and real objective existence, drawn from the very idea that humans have of him, has been strengthened despite Kant's criticism (against Descartes' illustration of the argument). Shedd vindicated this in his Dogmatic Theology of its true nature and value.[^1]

Despite Descartes' skepticism, he cannot doubt his existence from the fact that he thinks, thereby deducing God's existence to assure the reliability of human faculties and the certainty of knowledge. Descartes' arguments are given by Francis Beattie, such as the idea of an infinite God requires the postulate of God's existence, contingent forms require the assumption of a necessary being, and the inference that God's existence is implied from the *very idea* (innate) that the human mind indispensably has of him. We cannot think of an all-perfect being without its necessary existence. To make his existence contingent is not to think of a perfect being. According to Beattie, the force of Kant's criticism against the Cartesian proof may not be fatal if we see the real meaning of the illustration that Descartes used, namely, "any quality which is necessary to the completeness of the idea must be supposed to have reality."[^2] Annuling the predicate in the propositions "a triangle exists" or "God exists" leaves the fact that the mind possesses those ideas of perfect being and triangle unaffected. We cannot eradicate the very idea of God since it belongs to the nature of man.

The typical form of the ontological argument is found in Anselm's Proslogion, Apology, and Monologion in its *purest* and most effective form. The very idea that God is *of a being than whom a greater cannot be thought* is present in both theists and atheists objectively and subjectively. To defend Anselm from criticism, Beattie pointed out that Anselm used *cogitare* for "*think*" rather than *concipere* for "*conceive*." The very idea of God is a product of thought rather than a mere conception of the understanding.[^3] The objective existence of God is greater than the subjective existence in a human's idea. Contrary to Guanilo, who was Anselm's contemporary, the proof is unique and not a generalization (as the concept of an island). The correct interpretation of Anselm's proof, which is "*what is necessarily thought to exist in fact, does actually exist*" has a rational force, and cannot be discarded as circular reasoning. It does not assume God's existence; it argues that "since the idea of God in the mind implies the existence of its [necessary] object, the existence of God may be legitimately inferred from the very idea we have of him."[^4]

In Beattie's general estimate of this proof, he mentioned that the proof underlies a valid principle of inference where there lies a rational unity at the very root of both human thought and objective existence. Here, we can rest that there exists an objective and real knowledge of the external world grounded in the postulate of God.
