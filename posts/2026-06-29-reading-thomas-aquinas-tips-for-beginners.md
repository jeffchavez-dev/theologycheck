> "First and foremost is the need for a first-hand reading of Aquinas. I know of no evangelical critic of Thomistic theism who is a Thomistic scholar. Most criticism of Aquinas is based either on stereotyped textbook scholasticism or second-hand evangelical pseudo-scholarship. If others interpreted the Bible the way we have tried to understand Aquinas – through second-hand critical distortions – we would justly cry 'Unfair! Go back to the originals and understand them in context.' To this we add, 'Go thou and do likewise with the writings of Aquinas.'"
>
> — Norman Geisler[^1]

Some critics, particularly within Protestant and Reformed circles, are quick to dismiss Thomas Aquinas as merely a "papist," unworthy of serious attention. Some also see Reformed Thomism as an oxymoron. This reaction is often driven by a misunderstanding of Aquinas's project and its historical reception. Yet such dismissal overlooks the immense value of his careful reasoning, metaphysical clarity, and robust doctrine of God, many aspects of which were embraced, appropriated, and refined by the Protestant Scholastics themselves.

For those who wish to engage Aquinas deeply while remaining confessionally Protestant, Dr. David Haines offers helpful guidance. He encourages readers to approach Aquinas discerningly, appreciating what aligns with Scripture and the Reformed tradition while critically evaluating areas of doctrinal difference. His tips help readers avoid the twin errors of uncritical acceptance and knee-jerk rejection. Aquinas, though not Protestant, offers theological tools and philosophical insights that continue to enrich classical theism and natural theology today.[^2]

In this final section, I'm simply going to offer some quick tips for beginners that might save them from serious errors in their interpretation of Thomas Aquinas. Many of these tips could be applied to reading any author, including the Bible.

**1.** If you are reading another author to help you understand Thomas Aquinas, you should not (absolutely not) refer to an author who sees Thomas Aquinas as the enemy, or who disagrees completely (on anything) with Thomas Aquinas.[^3] If you want to understand Thomas Aquinas well, the best thing you could do is read Thomas Aquinas with the help of someone who agrees with what Thomas says, and understands him. If this is not possible for you, then you should refer to a book (commentary or introduction) that is written by an author who is a Thomist. The books I have suggested in this short treatise will be helpful for the beginner. It is best to have taken the time to understand Thomas Aquinas according to his own writings (and those who adhere to his thought) before you start reading those who would have criticized his thoughts. Both positions (for and against) are important, but it is better, for the understanding of the author in question, to start with those who are for than to start with those who are against.

**2.** Even if you are an exceptionally intelligent person, you should not start reading Thomas Aquinas thinking that you will easily understand what he says. If you really want to understand a person's thought (especially a thinker as important and nuanced as Thomas Aquinas), then you must begin reading their works with an attitude of humility. You must let them teach you before you can criticize them. It is the arrogance of our generation to think that we will be able to understand, at first superficial reading, the thoughts of a great thinker. It is better to start with the idea that Thomas Aquinas is much smarter than we are (after all, he is one of the most important thinkers in world history, and I am...). A "devout" Christian might want to say, "Yes, but, if he was not a true Christian, then his intelligence is worthless." Some comments do not deserve a response.

**3.** It would be a very good idea to find a book of Thomistic definitions. I might suggest, in English, the following book: Bernard J. Wuellner, *Summary of Scholastic Principles* (Chicago: Loyola University Press, 1956).

**4.** Don't jump to conclusions. Thomas Aquinas will say things that seem strange, incomprehensible, or even (at first glance) frightening. Don't jump to conclusions. You have to give the author the benefit of the doubt. That is, instead of immediately concluding: he is mistaken. Start with the conclusion: I must have misunderstood; and then seek to understand better (reread the section, read other writings by Thomas Aquinas on the same subject—he wrote several times on almost every subject, read commentators who discuss the section in question, talk with a specialist). After doing the research mentioned here, you will be better able to make a negative or positive judgment.

**5.** The last piece of advice I'll give here is that it's always a good thing when interpreting Thomas Aquinas (or any author) to understand the history of the development of human thought (both theological—Thomas Aquinas was a theologian—and philosophical), and where Thomas Aquinas (or any thinker) fits into that history. Understanding that history, as well as the terms that are used to name different periods of thought as well as different schools of thought, will help you better understand Thomas Aquinas (or any thinker) in his historical context, and in the context of the schools of thought that might influence him. Just to give an example of how one can go wrong in interpreting a thinker if one doesn't take into account his context in the history of the development of human thought: it would be a major mistake to say that Thomas Aquinas was a *rationalist*, or a *continental thinker*; even though he believed there was a place in theology for reasoning (e.g., we cannot interpret the Bible without using our intellect), even though he lived on the European continent. Generally, we use the term "rationalist" to refer to a philosopher (and theologian) who: (1) is/was influenced by the thought of René Descartes (c. 1596–1650), and (2) thinks that any truth (including the truths of Christianity) can be demonstrated by reason alone. Thomas Aquinas did not believe (2) at all; and it is obvious that he was not influenced by Descartes at all; so, we cannot call him a rationalist. Generally, when we talk about continental thinkers, we are referring to European philosophers, living on the continent (especially France and Germany) in the 19th and 20th centuries. It is obvious, then, that Thomas is not a "continental" philosopher. The point of all this is that we must know where Thomas stands in the history of the development of human thought (both theological and philosophical) if we are to understand Thomas's thought properly.[^3]

---

To put it succinctly, here's the summary:

- **Begin with Thomists. Don't start with critics.** Read Aquinas alongside interpreters who understand and affirm his thought. Critical engagement comes later—after you've grasped him fairly.[^4]
- **Read with humility.** Aquinas is not easy. Assume he knows more than you, and let him teach you before trying to judge or refute him.
- **Use a glossary.** Scholastic terms are precise and technical. A guide like Wuellner's *Summary of Scholastic Principles* will keep you from misreading key concepts.
- **Avoid quick judgments.** If something sounds wrong, assume you've misunderstood. Reread, compare passages, and consult faithful commentators before concluding anything. According to R.C. Sproul, he deserves the benefit of a second glance.[^5]
- **Know his context.** Aquinas is a 13th-century thinker shaped by both Christian theology and Aristotelian philosophy—not a Rationalist or modern Continental philosopher. Read him on his own terms, in his historical setting.[^6]

These aren't just tips for reading Thomas Aquinas—they're principles for engaging any serious theological thinker, whether it's Augustine, Anselm, the Reformers, or the Church Fathers. For Protestants especially, anyone interested in retrieving classical theism, metaphysics, or natural theology will find Aquinas an indispensable conversation partner.

Let's be clear: we're not exalting Aquinas above Scripture—nor are we canonizing him. You don't have to become Roman Catholic to benefit from his clarity, rigor, and enduring theological insight. I say this as someone who has been reading Aquinas and the secondary literature surrounding his work for some time—yet I remain joyfully and convictionally a Reformed Baptist.

*Soli Deo Trino Gloria!*
